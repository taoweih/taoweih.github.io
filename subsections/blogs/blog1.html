What Do Business Managers and Engineers Have In Common?

They may have more similarities than you think




November 10th, 2023


What do business managers and engineers have in common? You might say, “Nothing.” It’s understandable why people would think that. After all, business managers and engineers require very different skill sets. Engineers need a strong foundation in math and sciences, while business managers often rely on interpersonal and leadership skills. I used to share this belief. I thought the qualities needed for engineers and business managers were incompatible. However, I'd like to offer a different perspective. Engineers and business managers do something in common, and that key factor is abstraction.


My appreciation for the concept of abstraction began in one of my introductory computer engineering classes. In that class, we started by learning the construction of a computer from its most fundamental components. We all know that computers can only read 1s and 0s, but the class started even before that by teaching logic gates (basic operations like “and” and “or”). We then progressed to more complex concepts built upon these logic gates, followed by understanding how different components of a computer are created from these more complex elements. This is the first level of abstraction. When learning about how different components of a computer function, we essentially ignored individual logic gates. Considering a single component could consist of thousands of logic gates, it would be impossible for the human brain to focus on each one. Yet, we don’t need to focus on these small details. the power of abstraction allows us to achieve complexity without needing to know every detail. A modern computer chip contains billions of transistors, representing billions of logic gates—an order of magnitude beyond what was taught in an introductory computer engineering class. Abstraction is what enables the creation of such a complex chip.


So, what can business managers learn from this? Too often, managers attempt to control every detail, wanting to know precisely what’s happening with regular progress reports and frequent meetings. While it's essential for managers to be invested in a team's progress, excessive involvement can hinder employees’ productivity. In large companies, employees might spend more time in meetings than actually working. The analogy between managing and engineering is clear. If a manager fixates on small details, they lose sight of the bigger picture. Their primary role—leading on a large scale—suffers. By devoting time and attention to every detail of an employee's work, managers leave little room for effective management. Project details are crucial, but they are tasks for other team members. Just as computer programmers do not need to concern themselves with the intricacies of individual transistors, managers can operate efficiently through abstraction. Managers should familiarize themselves with the general concepts of their employees' work without micromanaging. It only hurts both their and employee’s productivity.


Abstraction proves invaluable for both business managers and engineers, as both professions are about solving problems. Abstraction allow them to have a clear sight of the most prominent problem, safeguarding them from distractions. Analytical skills geared toward the bigger picture are increasingly important, particularly in the face of advancing Artificial Intelligence technology. AI excels in managing details and repetitive tasks. Therefore, the ability to think abstractly is a crucial skill for navigating the future job market.
